# Who Makes Open Source Code? The Hybridisation of Commercial and Open Source Practices

**Authors:** Peter Mehler, Eva Iris Otto, Anna Sapienza (Copenhagen Center for Social
Data Science; Università del Piemonte Orientale)
**Venue:** *EPJ Data Science* (2024) 13:35
**DOI:** [10.1140/epjds/s13688-024-00475-0](https://doi.org/10.1140/epjds/s13688-024-00475-0)
**License:** Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 (CC BY 4.0) · Open Access
**Source:** [who-makes-open-source-code.pdf](../../../raw-input/papers/who-makes-open-source-code.pdf)

## What it is

A large-scale network analysis of the GitHub multi-language dependency graph, modelling
the **power and infrastructural dynamics of software production** — who the ecosystem
actually depends on, and how commercial and open-source actors interleave.

## Key findings

- A "small core of repositories delivers the majority of dependencies… the majority of
  GitHub projects rely on a small subset of the overall ecosystem" ([Mehler et al. 2024](../../../raw-input/papers/who-makes-open-source-code.pdf#page=15), p. 15) —
  "not all F/OSS repositories on GitHub are equal in terms of influence."
- This core is best read as a "GitHub-elite" — specialized by language and weighted toward
  **organizations** rather than individuals in ownership ([Mehler et al. 2024](../../../raw-input/papers/who-makes-open-source-code.pdf#page=15), p. 15).
- Open-source organizations and individuals provide the most *direct* dependencies, but
  large private companies (e.g. Microsoft, Google) are "generally better connected to other
  influential nodes," inserting themselves into the core — the **hybridisation** of
  commodified and open-source code ([Mehler et al. 2024](../../../raw-input/papers/who-makes-open-source-code.pdf#page=14), p. 14).
