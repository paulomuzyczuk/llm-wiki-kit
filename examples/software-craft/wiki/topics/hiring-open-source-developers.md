# Hiring Open-Source Developers

Hiring developers with open-source experience carries "a big advantage compared to hiring other
kinds of developers": most of an open-source developer's résumé "is public — it's everything
they've ever done in every open source project they've ever worked on, because all of that activity
is publicly archived"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=103), p. 90).
This page is the hiring-side payoff of the same public record that [[innersourcing]] forfeits (the
"portable résumé"): you can evaluate a candidate the way you would [[evaluating-open-source-projects]],
by reading their actual conduct.

## Ask candidates to point you at their public record

You "shouldn't need to go searching for all of it." In the job posting, "tell prospective candidates
directly that the résumé they send in should include references to their open source profile" — their
committer accounts on projects, "their account names at the overall project hosting sites," the
"email addresses or usernames they have used when posting in discussion forums, documentation they
have written, and anything else that would lead you to places where you can see their open source
project activity"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=103), p. 90).

## Read the social record, not just the technical one

"Look not only at their direct technical activity, but also at their relations with the other
developers in the project." Examine commits, "but also examine the frequency with which they
reviewed others' commits, and examine the candidate's reaction to reviews of their own commits."
In the issue tracker, "how often did the candidate respond constructively to incoming bug reports"?
In a threaded view of the forums, "see how often the candidate's posts were responded to, and what
the general tone of the responses was." The key inference: "someone who consistently causes negative
reactions from others in the project may have social problems as a collaborator, which is important
to know independently of their technical ability"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=103), p. 90).
The forensic move mirrors project evaluation — [[code-review]] participation and tone are visible and
diagnostic.

## Absence of experience is a question, not a verdict

If a candidate "seems to have little or no open source experience," that "is not necessarily a
showstopper, but it's a sign that you should ask some probing questions" and "expect some ramp-up
time." Youth or general inexperience makes the absence "easy to understand." But if the candidate
"has been a programmer for a while," especially if "they already have experience as a *user* of"
the software in question "and yet they have never participated much in that project except to
download and use it, then you should ask them … why." Using software without contributing is fine;
but if "you're hiring someone to be a participant in a project, and they already had a chance to be
and chose not to, that could imply a lack of intrinsic motivation to participate" — or there may be
benign reasons ("the candidate's prior management forbade them"). "Whatever the reasons are, you
should make sure you find out"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=103), p. 90); ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=104), p. 91).

## Hiring for influence — and its dual loyalty

It is "very common for companies to hire an open source developer precisely because of her existing
position in an open source project." Whether she "is the founder or leader" or "just has commit
access, … her ability to get things done in the upstream community is part of her value … often, it
is just as important as raw technical skill"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=104), p. 91).

There is "nothing wrong with purchasing influence in this way, as long as the employer understands
that the new employee will have **dual loyalty**." It is "inappropriate to ask the employee to take
actions that would harm her standing in the project." The manager "needs to be sensitive to this,
and to let the employee know that the door is open for discussion and pushback if she ever feels
she's being put into such a situation" — which is why managers who understand open source matter
(see the middle-management discussion in [[corporate-open-source-participation]]). It is "perfectly
fine for the employee to promote the company's technical interests in the project, and to do so
openly, as long as the proposals are compatible with the project's overall goals and the company
provides resources to support those proposals in a way that's sustainable for the project"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=104), p. 91).

**Influence is not transferable.** "Position and influence travel with the person, not with the
employer." The occasional exceptions — "corporate-driven projects where the balance of power among
competitors is especially important, or … standards bodies with formal organizational representation
policies," where "a governance committee seat may be reserved for a certain company" — still leave
informal influence dominant: "individuals may not be truly interchangeable in practice." Because the
influence walks out the door with the person, this "makes the recommendations in … 'Hire for the
Long Term' all the more important": such an employee "has a pretty good bargaining position." Put it
to work by "having her help onboard others into open source projects"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=104), p. 91).
