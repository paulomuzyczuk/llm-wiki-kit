# Treating Users as Participants

A project's contributor pool is grown one interaction at a time, from the users it already
has. Fogel's framing: "Each interaction with a user is an opportunity to get a new
participant." A user who "takes the time to post to one of the project's mailing lists, or to
file a bug report" has already signalled "more potential for involvement than most users (from
whom the project will never hear at all)" — so follow up: thank her for a bug report and ask
if she wants to try fixing it; if she flagged a documentation gap, ask if she'd write the
missing material. Most will demur, "but it doesn't cost much to ask, and every time you do, it
reminds the other listeners… that getting involved in the project is something anyone can do"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=156), p. 143).

The aim is not only new developers: "even training people to write good bug reports pays off
in the long run." Every constructive interaction widens the funnel
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=156), p. 143).

## Don't punish a bad bug report — educate it

Fogel's "personal pet peeve" is developers who "express anger at people who file well-intended
but vague bug reports." The angry reply ("What are we supposed to do with so little
information? Sheesh.") fails twice: it ignores "the user's point of view," and it ignores "the
effect such a reaction might have on all the other people watching the exchange." A newbie
"will not know how to write a bug report"; the right response is to "Educate them! And do it in
such a way that they come back for more"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=156), p. 143).

A constructive reply, written to the user's point of view, does three things: it expresses
sympathy, it *tells him how* to report (the exact version, OS, and "the exact text of the
error, with no omissions"), and it "offers a pointer to much more detailed… instructions for
reporting bugs." That last step "means… you have to have the document prepared in advance," and
ideally it "evolve[s] over time in response to the particular sorts of omissions… users tend to
make"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=156), p. 143).

## "The project runs on participation"

Standard bug-reporting instructions often invite the reporter to supply a patch. The purpose
is *not* to raise the patch-to-report ratio — capable fixers already know patches are welcome.
Its real purpose is "to emphasize to all readers, especially those new to the project or new to
free software in general, that the project runs on participation. In a sense, the project's
current developers are no more responsible for fixing the bug than is the person who reported
it." The goal is "to make every user realize that there is no innate difference between himself
and the people who work on the project — that it's a question of how much time and effort one
puts in, not a question of who one is"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=156), p. 143); ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=157), p. 144).

People "want to be listened to. Secondarily, they want their bugs fixed." You may not always
manage the latter quickly, but "the project as a whole can give them the former"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=156), p. 143).

## The one exception: genuinely rude users

The don't-respond-angrily rule "does not apply to rude users" — people who "show a sneering
contempt at the project" regardless of informational content. "Retaliatory rudeness should be
avoided toward people with whom the project has (or would like to have) a sustained
interaction. But when someone makes it clear from the start that he is going to be a fountain
of bile, there is no point making him feel welcome." Such cases are "fortunately quite rare,
and they are noticeably rarer in projects that make an effort to engage users constructively
and courteously from their very first interaction"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=157), p. 144).
