# Contributor Motivation

Understanding *why* people work on a free-software project is the prerequisite for every
people-management technique in [[delegation-in-open-source]], [[praise-and-criticism]], and
[[treating-users-as-participants]]: "Understanding people's true motivations will help you
arrange things so as to attract and keep them"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=150), p. 137).

## Beyond the paycheck

Even when contributing *is* someone's job — their manager asked them to — "most participants
have some degree of intrinsic motivation that goes beyond a mere management request." Fogel's
managerial observation: "people are much more successful when they have their own motivations
for wanting to succeed than when they are merely performing work in return for a paycheck,"
and the vast majority of open-source developers "are not in it only for the paycheck"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=149), p. 136).

The stated reasons — wanting to produce good software, wanting to fix the bugs that matter to
them — are real but "usually not the whole story." The tell is a thought experiment: could
you imagine a participant "staying with a project even if no one ever said a word in
appreciation of her work, or listened to her in discussions? Of course not." People spend
time on free software "for reasons beyond just an abstract desire to produce good code"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=149), p. 136).

## The social driver: respect through cooperation

Alongside the challenge and educational value of hard problems, "humans also have a built-in
desire to work with other humans, and to give and earn respect through cooperative
activities." This is the lever a project runs on, but it has a structural requirement:
"Groups engaged in cooperative activities must evolve norms of behavior such that status is
acquired and kept through actions that help the group's goals"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=150), p. 137).

This is the same "influence ∝ contribution" ethic that defines [[open-source-culture]] — here
stated as a *design constraint* on the project's social system rather than a description of it.

## Norms don't arise by themselves

Left untended, a project can evolve the *wrong* status norms. On some projects "people
apparently feel that status is acquired by posting frequently and verbosely" — and they reach
that conclusion honestly, "because they are rewarded with respect for making long, intricate
arguments, whether or not that actually helps the project"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=150), p. 137).

The whole managerial task that follows is therefore framed as "creating an atmosphere in
which status-acquiring actions are also constructive actions" — aligning the respect people
seek with the work the project needs. Delegation, feedback, de-territorialization, and user
recruitment are all instruments of that alignment.

## Attention is the currency

What contributors are ultimately paid in is *attention*. Fogel states it flatly in the
context of why patches must not be ignored: "The true currency of open source projects is
attention: people who can see that they are getting attention will keep participating, even
if not every patch they submit lands"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=159), p. 146).

This currency is *scarce in a specific way*. The project's code can be copied by anyone, but
"attention, credibility, and influence in the project very much are" a finite shared resource:
"they are by definition not copyable, and therefore not forkable"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=149), p. 136).
That non-copyability is why these motivations have force — and why [[forkability]], which
caps every other kind of project power, does *not* dissolve the competition for standing.
