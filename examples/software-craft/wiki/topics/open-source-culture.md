# Open-Source Culture

Open source is "a culture by choice." Its participants are bound by no contract and often
have "never actually met the other participants face-to-face"; the bonds that normally
hold a group together "are narrowed down to a tiny channel: the written word, carried over
electronic wires" ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=21), p. 8).
Because exit is free — "if people are unhappy in one project, they will just wander off to
another one" — coercive techniques don't work, and a project can "lose a potential
participant in the first five minutes" if it makes a poor first impression
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=21), p. 8).
Managing such a community is the art of keeping voluntary participants choosing to stay.

## The cohesion principle: influence proportional to contribution

The single sentence Fogel offers as the distillation of the whole book:

> "People should feel that their connection to a project, and influence over it, is
> directly proportional to their contributions." ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=21), p. 8)

The corollary is that "no class of developers… should ever feel discounted or
discriminated against for non-technical reasons" — which puts corporate-sponsored or
salaried-developer projects on special notice, since uneven status is precisely what
erodes the voluntary commitment of everyone else ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=21), p. 8).
This is the same ethic that [[free-software-vs-open-source]] frames as *the contribution
trumps the contributor*, and that [[open-source-governance]] turns into concrete voting and
maintainership rules.

## Management is real, just subtle

A common fallacy is "that little or no project management is required in open source," or
that in-house practices transfer unchanged. In fact a project is "a random collection of
programmers… who have most likely never met each other," and a thought experiment shows
that without management "it would collapse or drift apart very quickly"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=15), p. 2).
The management is "often informal and subtle": its goal is mostly to sustain the shared
belief "that they can do more in concert than individually" — by setting communication
standards, keeping useful developers from being marginalized, and "making the project a
place developers want to keep coming back to" ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=15), p. 2).

## Open source is not a panacea

Opening a project is not a free win and not a cure for a troubled one — "quite the
opposite: opening up a project can add whole new sets of complexities, and cost more in the
short term than simply keeping it in-house." It means making code "comprehensible to
complete strangers," writing development docs, and standing up collaboration tools, all of
which is "pure overhead at first" ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=14), p. 1).
A related, recurring mistake is **skimping on presentation and packaging** — treating it as
deferrable busywork. Its benefits are "most visible to those least familiar with the
project," so developers who already know how to build and install undervalue it; Fogel
insists it "be a priority from the very start" ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=15), p. 2).

## Failures of cultural navigation

A recognizable global free-software culture now exists — "not monolithic" but with "a
basically consistent core" of rewarded and punished behaviors that longtime participants
have internalized. Unsuccessful projects "usually deviate in significant ways from this
core, albeit unintentionally," so when conflict arrives "they lack an already established
stock of cultural reflexes to fall back on" ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=15), p. 2).
A structural sub-case: some organizations fit open source worse than others. Fogel reports
that, on the whole, "governments are less suited to participating in free software projects
than for-profit corporations are, with non-profits somewhere in between" — and that a
"hierarchical, risk-averse, and publicity-sensitive" organization joining a project "will
usually have to make some adjustments" ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=15), p. 2).
The countervailing benefit is the project as "a membrane through which your managers and
developers are regularly exposed to people and ideas outside your organizational hierarchy"
— "like… attending a conference" while still getting daily work done ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=15), p. 2).
