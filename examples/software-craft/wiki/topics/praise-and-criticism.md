# Praise and Criticism

Giving feedback is a deliberate management instrument, not an emotional reflex. Fogel's
governing insight is that the two seeming opposites are mechanically alike: "Praise and
criticism are not opposites; in many ways, they are very similar. Both are primarily forms of
attention, and are most effective when specific rather than generic. Both should be deployed
with concrete goals in mind"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=151), p. 138).
Both are ways of spending the project's scarce currency, [[contributor-motivation|attention]].

## Both are diluted by inflation

"Praise too much or too often and you will devalue your praise; the same is true for
criticism" — though in practice criticism, being "usually reactive," is "a bit more resistant
to devaluation"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=151), p. 138).
The discipline is to keep both *scarce enough to mean something*.

## Detailed, dispassionate criticism reads as praise

A defining feature of technical culture: "detailed, dispassionate criticism is often taken as
a kind of praise… because of the implication that the recipient's work is worth the time
required to analyze it." But "both of those conditions — detailed and dispassionate — must be
met for this to be true"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=151), p. 138).
This is the same dynamic [[written-communication]] records under "recognizing rudeness": close
criticism is a compliment; *skimping* on promised criticism is the real slight.

## Criticize the work, never the person

When a change is wrong, "That was sloppy" is "useless (and actually harmful)," because
"Sloppiness is ultimately a characteristic of a person, not of their work." Keep reactions
"focused on the work": describe what is wrong "tactfully and without malice." If it is the
third or fourth careless change in a row, it is fair to say so — "again without anger" — "to
make it clear that the pattern has been noticed"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=151), p. 138).

Crucially, escalation is not the remedy for someone who does not improve: "the solution is not
more or stronger criticism. The solution is for the group to remove that person from the
position of incompetence, in a way that minimizes hurt feelings as much as possible" — the
subject of Transitions (Ch. 8). That outcome is rare; "most people respond pretty well to
criticism that is specific, detailed, and contains a clear (even if unspoken) expectation of
improvement"
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=151), p. 138); ([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=152), p. 139).

## Praise is a tool — use it sparingly and on purpose

Praise "won't hurt anyone's feelings," but that is exactly why it should be used "no less
carefully than criticism." Before praising, "ask yourself why you want to use it." The rule:
do not regularly praise people "for doing what they usually do, or for actions that are a
normal and expected part of participating" — once you start, "it would be hard to know when to
stop," and "repeated praise for normal behavior gradually becomes meaningless anyway." Reserve
praise for "unusual or unexpected efforts, with the intention of encouraging more such
efforts," and when someone's productivity rises permanently, "adjust your praise threshold for
that person accordingly" so the new level is treated as the normal baseline
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=152), p. 139).

## Acknowledgement without direct praise

Recognition need not be explicit praise. If the project is set up right, "everything that
person does is already visible anyway." Alternatives that acknowledge work indirectly:
mention in passing that she is "the resident expert" in an area, "publicly consult her on some
question about the code," or — "perhaps most effectively" — "conspicuously make further use of
the work she has done," so she sees others relying on her results. Someone who makes large
contributions "will occupy a position of influence by default"; explicit steps are needed only
when a contributor seems underappreciated
([Fogel 2023](../../raw-input/books/producing-open-source-software/producing-open-source-software.pdf#page=152), p. 139).
