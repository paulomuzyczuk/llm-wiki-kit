# Why it's shaped this way — the theory behind the contract

The contract's more opinionated rules aren't arbitrary — several are deliberate borrowings from library, archival, and information science, disciplines that have spent more than a century on the problem this project runs into immediately: how do you keep a *growing* collection findable, trustworthy, and honest about its own limits? This page walks through the lineage behind four of the design choices people most often ask about, the single tradeoff that ties them together, and one blunt clarification about what the system is and isn't. The README carries a short version; this is the long one.

## Negative space is recorded, not silently absent

Every page documents what was deliberately *not* written up, drawn from a fixed vocabulary of rejection categories — `too-granular`, `subsumed-by`, `out-of-scope`, `foil`, `source-underdeveloped`, and so on. In archival science this act has a name: **appraisal**, the judgement about what carries enduring value and what doesn't. The field's hard-won lesson is that appraisal is both unavoidable (nobody preserves everything in full) and never neutral (what you keep is shaped by your moment, your purpose, and your blind spots), so the professional discipline is not to pretend selection didn't happen but to *make it visible and accountable*. Fiorella Foscarini's survey of appraisal theory in *Currents of Archival Thinking* (2017) presses exactly this point: because any attribution of value is "time-bound and culturally conditioned," the decision and its rationale must be documented so that a later reader can audit it rather than inherit it blind. Most note systems record only what made the cut, and the omissions vanish without a trace — which means you can never tell whether a topic is absent because it was judged out of scope, already covered elsewhere, or simply never reached. By writing the rejection *and its category* directly onto the page, the framework converts an invisible editorial judgement into a reviewable one, and hands the linter and the human reviewer something concrete to check against.

## Citations are surrogate records, not just links

A citation here is two-part — a `#page=N` anchor into the source PDF and a `p. M` printed-page tail — and it sits alongside a `**Source entities:** [[<slug>-book]]` link to a dedicated page for the work itself. That whole structure comes straight from cataloguing theory. A catalogue entry is a **surrogate record**: a compact stand-in that lets you find, identify, and reason about a resource without holding the resource in your hands, and Elaine Svenonius's *The Intellectual Foundation of Information Organization* (2000) together with Joudrey & Taylor's *The Organization of Information* (2018) spell out the objectives such a surrogate must serve — among them *collocation* (gathering everything derived from one work in a single place) and a stable *identity* (a name for the work that survives changes in where and how it's stored). A raw file path fails that identity test the same way a URL with a server name baked into it does: it is a *location-embedded* identifier, so the instant the file moves, the edition is swapped, or the folder tree is reorganised, every citation pointing at it silently rots. The `[[<slug>-book]]` entity page is the location-independent name that replaces it — Tim Berners-Lee's "cool URIs don't change" applied to a vault — and it doubles as the collocation point where every claim drawn from that book can be gathered and cross-checked. The split page reference is the same instinct one level down: `#page=N` is the *manifestation coordinate* (where the sentence physically lands in this particular PDF) while `p. M` is the *edition-stable* printed page a reader of any copy can turn to, and because the two halves are grounded independently, a digitisation quirk in one can't quietly corrupt the other.

## A human reviews what the machine describes

The single hardest task in organizing information is **subject analysis** — deciding what a document is actually *about* — and it is hard because "aboutness" cannot simply be read off the surface of the text. Svenonius (2000) and Joudrey & Taylor (2018) both treat automated subject assignment as a long-standing open problem: machines do well where language is determinate and poorly on exactly the abstract, multi-faceted, or contested material that matters most, and as Svenonius observes, where a human intelligence cannot unequivocally classify a term, a machine can hardly be expected to do better. An LLM is a far more capable describer than the statistical indexers that literature had in view, but it belongs to the same family — a *computational* describer that can misjudge aboutness fluently and with great confidence. That is precisely why the framework refuses to let the writer also be the judge of its own work: `book-review` runs as a mandatory, independent check after each chapter is ingested, and the human stays the curator with final say. The division of labour is deliberate and load-bearing — the LLM *proposes* the synthesis, the citations, and the subject tags; an independent reviewer and the human *dispose*. The negative-space discipline above is part of the same safeguard, because it forces the describer to declare not only what it captured but what it chose to set aside, and a confident-but-wrong omission is the easiest error to miss when no one is required to look for it.

## Nothing is deleted; stale pages are archived

When a page falls out of date it is marked `status: archived` rather than removed, and the original source in `raw-input/` is treated as immutable. This mirrors a genuine shift in archival thinking. The older **lifecycle** model pictured a record moving through stages toward a terminal disposition — ultimately kept or destroyed — whereas the **records-continuum** model, originated by Frank Upward and summarised by Gillian Oliver in *Currents of Archival Thinking* (2017), reframes recordkeeping as a set of overlapping, continuous processes in which "disposition" is a transition between states, not an endpoint. For a *digital* collection the argument for retention is stronger still: storage is cheap, and aggressively pruning volume introduces selection bias while foreclosing kinds of later analysis the original curator could never have anticipated — so "keep it, but mark it superseded" almost always dominates "throw it away." Archiving rather than deleting also protects the provenance trail: a future reader can see not just what the vault currently holds true but how that belief changed over time, and that history is itself a form of evidence.

## The tradeoff underneath all four

Underneath all four choices sits one tradeoff that Robert Glushko's *The Discipline of Organizing* (2013) places at the centre of the field: the effort you invest in organizing a collection "on the way in" — describing, classifying, citing, cross-linking — is what gets repaid as findability and trust "on the way out," when someone actually searches it; skimp on the former and you pay for it in the latter, and the reverse holds too. Traditional libraries had to ration that upfront effort because skilled human cataloguers are scarce and expensive, which is why so much organizing work was historically deferred to search time. The framework's bet is that an LLM rewrites that economics: it can afford to describe, cite, and cross-reference every page to a standard a human maintaining notes by hand rarely sustains, so it deliberately spends "on the way in" to keep the vault honest and navigable "on the way out."

## What this is — and isn't

It's worth being blunt about one thing this system is *not*. It is a **reference base**, not a thinking tool, and the two have opposite theories of where the value lives. In the classic note-taking tradition — Sönke Ahrens's *How to Take Smart Notes* (2017), building on Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten — the payoff comes from *you* doing the writing. Rephrasing a source in your own words, working out how a new idea connects to what you already hold, and filing it is **elaboration**, and elaboration is the mechanism by which human memory and understanding actually form; on that account the slip-box is valuable less as a storehouse than as a discipline that *forces* that mental work on you every time you use it. A system in which an *LLM* reads the sources and writes the syntheses hands that workout to the model rather than to you. What it produces is genuinely valuable — a dense, cross-linked, well-cited body of knowledge optimised for **retrieval** — but querying it is not the same act as building it, and it will not teach you the material the way writing your own notes would. The honest framing is the one Andrej Karpathy uses: a "distilled wiki," a compressed store of understanding to draw on later, rather than a substitute for the thinking that produces understanding in the first place. Your own learning still has to come from reading the sources and from actively elaborating on whatever the vault hands back when you ask it something. Held to that expectation, it makes your exchanges with Claude sharper and better grounded; mistaken for a replacement for thinking, it will quietly disappoint.
